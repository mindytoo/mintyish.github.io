Projects by Mindy Tieu

GNOME
Spring 2015

The final product of our User Oriented Collaborative Design course, GNOME is a rugid, practical, and friendly introduction of robots into the world of small scale community farmers. By designing along side our users, our team was able to create a prototype for a small garden drone that gathers data on soil composition and protects gardens from pests, effectively arming small farmers with tools to produce efficiently like big company farms without sacrificing their values.

"Beer" and "Coffee" Heat Exchanger
Spring 2015

Inspired by a lecture by Professor Brian Story about a magical heat exchanger that takes in hot beer and cold coffee, our team decided to explore the thermodynamics behind a parallel heat exchanger by building and testing a similar set up as a small side project. Our final product: a humorous video describing our adventure building a heat exchanger.

Thermodynamics of a Putt Putt Boat
Spring 2015

As an exploration of maritime propulsion, our team analysed the thermodynamic processes of the Lenoir cycle occuring within the classic toy, the putt-putt boat. In order to fully understand the quirky propulsion mechanism of the boat, we created both a boat simulation in MATLAB and a small wooden putt-putt boat. Though our attempt at building a physical model of a putt-putt boat failed, we were able to vary the diameter of the piping as well as the number of candles on board to maximize the efficiency of work out to heat in of the Lenoir cycle.

Kinetic Sculpture Simulation
Fall 2014

Kinetic art is a piece that relies on its percieved motion for effect on the viewer. An integral part of many modern three dimensional kinetic sculptures are modeified pendulums, as exemplified in Ken Patton's "Thor's Hammers". By calculating the forces, moments, and accelerations acting in a modified quintuple compound pendulum, our team derived ordinary differential equations of motion for the system and implemented them in MATLAB to modelt he motion of the sculpture. For validation purposes, we deliberately ignored damping forces and were able to get constant total energy values for all sets of initial conditions. The addition of damping forces causes significant changes in system behavior but also shows a predictable decline in the total energy of the system.

"Snot Bot"
Fall 2014

Researching under Drew Bennett at Olin College and partnering with Ocean Alliance, the "snot bot" research team is sending autonomous quad-copters out to sea to find, follow, and collect blow (snot) samples from whales. Our goal is to allow for a faster, easier, and more efficient way to uninvasively collect important research samples. These copters are aimed to be intelligent enough to allow for taking off and landing autonomously, following a whale (or reaching pre-set way points), and landing back on the moving boat safely with samples.
