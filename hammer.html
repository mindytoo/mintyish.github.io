"Thor's Hammers" Mathematical Simulation of Kinetic Sculptures

The Project

Kinetic art is a piece that relies on its percieved motion for effect on the viwer. An integral part of many moden three dimensional kinetic sculptures are modified pendulums, as exemplified in Ken Patton's "Thor's Hammers". By calculating the forces, moments, and accelerations acting in a modified quintuple compound pendulum, fellow student, Maire Keene and I derived ordinary differential equations of motion for the system and implemented them in MATLAB to model the motion of the sculpture. For validation purposes, we deliberately ignored damping forces and were able to get constant total energy values for all sets of initial conditions. The addition of damping forces causes significant changes in system behavior but also shows a predictable decline in the total energy of the system.

The Simulation

Many dynamics courses review the case of a double pendulum, first using point masses and then evolving into compound rods. In reality one can find many examples of even more complex pendulums or systems that can be modeled well by multi-bodied pendulums. Here we have the familiar compund double pendulum modified into a planar quintuple pendulum composed of more complex bodies concatenated with pivots offset from the ends of those bodies. Our approach to the model involves applying Newton's Second Law and Euler's equations to each body. A simulation and animation of our system model was created and energy values for conditions where damping forces are assumed to be zero are used for validation.

The system model is based off of the kinetic sculpture Thor's Hammers", which is a set of five hammers concatenated using bearings and given some initial displacement and velocity. Note that the fifth hammer in the sculpture is modified to have two hammer heads, one at each end of the rod. Given the thin nature of the hammers, the expected speeds of the bodies, and the use of mechanical bearings in the sculpture, we are assuming that the only damping force is viscous damping and not aerodynamic damping. However, since our validation graphs rely on conservation of energy, we have set the damping coeficient to 0 in the simulation code used to produce the figures below. We also assume that the hammer handles can be abstracted into thin rod masses and that the hammer heads can be abstracted to point masses. This somewhat neglects a more accurate distribution of mass through the bodies, but is a better model of a hammer than a simple rigid rod such as used previously in our course pedulum models.
