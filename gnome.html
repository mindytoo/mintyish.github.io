GNOME The Garden Roving Drone

The Project

For the User Oriented Collaborative Design course at Olin College, I and five other students were assigned to create a final product that would improve the lives of the user group "community oriented farmers and gardeners." However, in order to create a product that they would find useful, we had to develope an understanding of our users and explore their routines and connections with their fellow gardeners, their gardens, and their community. After weeks of researching, interviewing, and interating with our users, our team developed GNOME, a garden rover that would assist in protection against intruders and collection of garden health data.

Understanding the Users

Originally, each team member had a different understanding of what the term "community gardener" meant. Thus we cast our net wide and conducted interviews with anyone who associated themselves with the term. What we quickly came to realize was that "community farmers" identified themselves as not having the same luxery of not having to live off their produce as "community gardeners" who thought of planting as an enjoyable hobby. We separated our users into "personas", the archetypal independent, rugged farmer, environmental and spiritual idealist, and technologically savvy free spirit.

Themes of independence and self reliance as well as small, local business models and environmental sustainability became apparent among the user values. A focus on manual labor and taking responsibility made us realize how much "community oriented farmers and gardeners" value hard work and innovative thinking. Uncovering these insights into our users lead allowed for us to design a solution that would not only help to solve common problems but empower them to maintain these idealogies.

The Design

Filtering through our product ideas, we found one that we felt enhanced the control users had over their land while being uniquely outfitted to small scale gardens and farms. GNOME is a small drone that follows prompts from users either by voice or by phone app. It is outfitted with cameras and a sensor that can detect highly variable soil conditions like pH and water content. It rolls around using technology pioneered by Cubli and the Institute for Dynamic Systems and Control. A series of rubber traction grips around the truncated icosahedron contribute to its rugged and outdoor character. On the opposite panels of the sensors and cameras are solar panels that appeal to the environmentally conscious.
